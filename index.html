DOES SILICON VALLEY HAVE AN ARROGANCE PROBLEM?
MAY 4, 2014

Identify a problem facing the tech world, Explain why it is a problem and some background, Hypothesize about potential solutions

-DBC Prompt

Last year the Wall Street Journal published an article called “Silicon Valley Has an Arrogance Problem.” The author documents and argues against what he believes is a superiority complex among techies. I grew up and worked in what the author calls the “shiny bubble” and I have mixed feelings about his article. Many of the techies I’ve worked with have been uniquely empathic and kind. I’ve been amazed by how some of the smartest people I know are also the most humble. But on the other hand, I’ve also seen a lot of the douchebaggery alluded to in the article. More than a few techies seem to obsess over status (number of employees, what street an office is located, what college a founder graduated from) the way middle school girls obsess over hairbands and Uggs.
A few years ago I had the opportunity to tour a prominent tech startup. When being shown the main office I noticed two employees, a designer and a programmer, arguing. They were debating how light refraction would affect one of their hardware prototypes. The designer made a reasonable point about the risk involved, to which the programmer responded, “I didn’t realize they taught physics at the Academy of Art.”
The point I want to make is that tech is the same as any other industry: you have some good eggs and some bad ones. Tech is just under a brighter spotlight right now, and the bad eggs tend to be louder and say more news-worthy things than the good ones.
I don’t have a solution for arrogance. I can only say that I hope people do notice the good eggs and try to emulate them.


HOW TO SWEEP GLOBAL VARIABLES UNDER THE CARPET
MAY 4, 2014

Global variables. At first you don’t mind them, they take up a couple lines at the top of your program but aren’t hurting anyone. But as your program gets more and more complex you need more and more of them. Next thing you know you have a heinous mass of arrays and hashes taking up a hundred lines of your otherwise beautiful code.
Global variables are ugly: they’re in ALL CAPS, a jumbled eyesore. Back when I first started embedded systems programming they cluttered my code to an embarrassing degree. I finally had the sense to put them in a separate file. Suddenly everything was pretty again. Fortunately, loading a Ruby file from another Ruby file is super easy. All it takes is one little line of code:

require_relative ‘alotta_ugly_variables’

Plop your hideous crap into a second file, load it, and no one will ever has to look at your mess again.

-DBC Prompt

Well, I didn’t see that one coming. These Dev Bootcamp prompts never fail to surprise.
I’m generally a conflict-free person. The last time I got in trouble was in Kindergarten, and that was for pranks, not acts of violence or aggression. In fifth grade I was a badge-wearing, clipboard-toting Conflict Mediator (I was eventually removed in a coup d’etat. Admittedly I was drunk off my own power). Now in college I’m a Peer Counselor, so people expect me to be temperate, soft-spoken, and agreeable (I’m at least one of these three things).
Every “conflict” I’ve had in my life has been between me and my brother during childhood. I love my brother, but he was the kind of kid who took the TV remote with him to the bathroom so you couldn’t change the channel. I’m neat and organized, he’s slobbish and unkempt.
Most of my memories of conflict with my brother have been blocked, thanks to the miracles of modern science, but I do recall one time in particular. My parents were out of town, and it was just me and him at home for the weekend. I was probably about twelve. My brother was being particularly tyrannical with the TV remote and I was in a bad mood. At one point during his One Tree Hill binge he left to go get a snack, leaving the remote unguarded for a full thirty seconds. I snuck down into the TV room, turned the TV to infomercials, removed one of the remote’s AA batteries, and snuck back out. He went through at least three or four hours of sweaty panic before he thought to check the battery compartment. He put two and two together and was less that happy about my trick. Long story short, he manages to lock me out of the house. While pressing his face up to a window and taunting me I punched him through the glass.

What was the source of the conflict?

-DBC Prompt

The source of the conflict, I suppose, was One Tree Hill. Those shows really aren’t healthy. Or perhaps it was two antithetical boys trapped in a crucible of boredom. I prefer to blame the former.

LETS HASH IT OUT: ARRAYS VS. HASHES
APRIL 27, 2014

Arrays vs. Hashes. What are they? What are they good for? How do you know which one to use?

-DBC Prompt

An array is an ordered, integer-indexed list of elements/objects. An array is essentially a collection of unique values, be they integers, strings or any other data type. Indexing starts at zero, which is annoying if you’re not used to it (you will be).
Back in my Java days (oh, the things I’ve seen…) we did a lot of work with multi-dimensional arrays. If a one-dimensional array is a list, then a two-dimensional array is a grid. 2D arrays were great for things like coordinates. It wasn’t long ago that I wanted to use a 2D array in a Ruby project. I was surprised to find that 2D arrays don’t exist in Ruby, though as with anything in life, there are ways to get around it. In Ruby, you can create Inception-Arrays, that is, arrays within arrays. For example:

a = [[0,1,2], [3,4,5]]

So next time you’re at a cocktail party and some snobby Java programmer tells you Ruby can’t do two dimensional arrays, you know what to say. Also, find better cocktail parties to go to.
In contrast to arrays, which are but simple lists, hashes are key/value pairs. Objects are in groups of two. This is really useful when storing things like usernames and passwords. Instead of having two arrays (or even more confusingly, a hacky 2D array), one for usernames and one for passwords, you can use a hash to link each username with the corresponding password.


FROM PROGRAMMER TO BROGRAMMER
APRIL 20, 2014

If as many people are programming as would seem to be programming, then it would be inexcusable to program alone.

-Tennessee Williams (paraphrased)

Programmers have a bad reputation when it comes to working with others. In many cases this is merited (boy do I have some stories), but the best programmers know how to work in teams. I’m glad that DBC is starting us early on group programming.
I’ve enjoyed working with other DBC students thus far. We’re a pretty fun bunch.
One thing that’s been great about working with others is that we’re able to fill in each others’ gaps in knowledge. When I don’t know something generally another student will, and when someone I’m working with (such as in this week’s GPS) doesn’t know something I’m able to help out.
I noticed that there is definite caution to the way students interact. Everything is posed as a tentative question, such as “Would it be OK with you if I navigated now?” This isn’t a bad thing, it is good that everyone’s polite, but I think knowing that we are constantly under review changes how we interact and adds a bit of pressure.
I’m honestly not crazy about DBC’s feedback system. It is purportedly anonymous, but it is always clear who’s giving you feedback based on the timing. Don’t get me wrong, I’m happy to be critiqued. My discomfort comes more from when I have to write feedback for others. It’s uncomfortable to have to tell others what their character flaws are. In the DBC system we’re required to spend half our review on what the reviewee did wrong. My partner in my first GPS was great, I honestly didn’t want him to change how he interacted with team member. However, I had to come up with something he did wrong per the feedback prompt.
I would prefer it if feedback was left to the instructors. They’re present for the entire session and can focus on the student interactions, freeing us to focus on the assignment.


POPULARITY IS A SYNTACTICALLY-TRANSMITTED-DISEASE
APRIL 20, 2014

Why is JavaScript so popular?

-Dev Bootcamp Prompt

People have been hating on JavaScript for a long time. Anyone who reads Hacker News some semblance of regularity knows that programmers love to write about how much JavaScript sucks. And in many ways they have a point. Compared to other languages like Python and Ruby it is ugly. That’s why languages that compile to JavaScript, like CoffeeScript, exist. They want to make scripts shorter and prettier. I used CoffeeScript for a little while and really liked it, but the problem is that its not the standard. JavaScript is. JavaScript’s biggest strength is that pretty much everyone uses it for almost every website.
Another reason why JavaScript is popular is that its fairly easy to use. Sure, the creators of CoffeeScript would probably disagree, but anyone who has waded through C or VB state machines (the horror!) knows that code can get a lot uglier than JVS.
A big part of usability is tool diversity. There are TONS of tools to program in JavaScript. While this is perhaps a result of popularity (more people using javascript means more customers for people who make programming tools) it also feeds popularity. Programmers, like everybody else, like to have options. The more options available the more incentive there is to learn a language.
Another reason why JavaScript is popular is that it can be edited, modified, and tested really, really quickly. I’ve spent years programming hardware, and testing new code means compiling it in an editor, uploading it to a microprocessor and powering up the device. Depending on the compiler it could take up to a minute to test new code. Programming involves lots of testing, so this ate up a huge amount of time. With JavaScript I can write a new block of code and test it in just a couple seconds.
That’s pretty awesome. JavaScript, for better or worse, will continue to be the standard for the foreseeable future. Many gripe about this, but believe me, it could be a lot worse.


PUTTING THE "CLASS" IN "CLASS"
APRIL 14, 2014

What are the best practices associated with using classes vs. ids?

-Dev Bootcamp Prompt

When I joined my first Guided Pairing Session with Greg and Ariel I relized I had only been using classes up to that point. I had never once used an id. This was technically working for me. None of my css files were too complicated so I could create a class for a single element without any negative consequences. However as Ariel explained to me, this isn't an ideal practice long-term. Ids are for single elements, whereas classes are for groups of them. They are defined slightly differently (ids have hash tags when being defined in CSS) but attributes are specified the same way. I've since found myself using ids a lot more, and I think my code is the better for it.
